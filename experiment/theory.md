### INTRODUCTION<br>

<p style="text-indent:50px; text-align: justify;">Infiltration is defined as the rate at which water enters the soil, whereas percolation refers to the rate at which water moves through the soil. Percolation also indicates the soil's capacity to absorb water. The quantity of water absorbed by a soil over a specified period is termed the percolation rate. This parameter plays a significant role in the design of filter fields and in assessing the suitability of a site for on-site wastewater treatment systems. Since percolation depends on soil characteristics, different types of soil exhibit different percolation rates.</p>

<p style="text-indent:50px; text-align: justify;">Particle size plays a important role in determining the percolation rate of soil. As particle size decreases, the surface area of soil grains increases, which reduces the rate of percolation. Conversely, soils with larger particle sizes allow faster percolation.</p>

<p style="text-indent:50px; text-align: justify;">In addition to particle size, the shape of soil particles also influences percolation. Rounded particles facilitate easier movement of water compared to irregularly shaped particles. Another important factor is the surface texture of the particles: soils with smooth-surfaced particles exhibit higher percolation rates than those with rough surfaces.</p>

<p style="text-indent:50px; text-align: justify;">For experimental purposes, the percolation rate is determined by measuring the quantity of water passing through a test hole over a specified period of time. In general, the percolation rate is highest in sandy soils and lowest in clayey soils, indicating that percolation is influenced by particle size.

A major limitation of this test is that it does not account for capillary action. The percolation rate is commonly expressed as the amount of water (mL) per unit time (minutes), denoted as Q.</p>

<p style="text-indent:50px; text-align: justify;">The percolation rate test is used to determine the water absorption capacity of soil and plays a significant role in the design of filter fields and in evaluating site suitability for on-site wastewater treatment systems. However, a major limitation of this test is that it does not account for capillary action.</p>

#### Applications:
The percolation test provides valuable information about the soil's ability to absorb and transmit water. This information is essential for designing on-site wastewater treatment systems, filter beds, and drainage structures, ensuring proper water movement and preventing waterlogging. Additionally, the test helps evaluate the suitability of sites for groundwater recharge, construction projects, and environmental protection by predicting how quickly water or contaminants may percolate through the soil. Overall, it is a key tool in groundwater engineering for assessing soil hydraulic properties and planning sustainable water management practices.
